- The decline of the Liberal Party and the rise of the Labour Party are key developments in c20th British political history
- Liberal Party's decline from 400 MPs in 1906 to 40 in 1924, and Labour's growth from 2 in 1900 to 191 in 1923, are well-documented facts
- But historians debate the reasons for and timing of this change
#### Causes of change
- Class Politics explanation: Led by **Ross McKibbin**
- Suggests Labour's increasing association with the trade union movement captured working-class support, leading to the decline of the Liberal Party
- Liberal 'revisionists', including **Trevor Wilson** and **Michael Bentley** argue that WW1 exposed the Liberal Party's ideological weaknesses and created internal tensions that led to its decline

#### Debate and Perspectives
- Have been several sub-debates, including the impact of the pre-war parliamentary franchise, the New Liberal ideology, and local developments
- Some historians, such as **Matthew**, **McKibbin**, and **Kay** argue that the pre-war franchise exluded many working-class men, which would have given labour a stronger challenge to Liberalism.
- Others like **P.F. Clarke**, **M. Hart** and **Duncan Tanner** disagree and suggest the war caused the Liberals to break up intellectually
- New Liberal ideology, which emphasised social reform, is seen as a key factor in the Liberals' decline or survival, depending on the historian's perspective

#### Franchise and Voting
- Pre-war franchise imposed random inequality and denied the vote to male members of both the middle and working classes
- At least 350,000 single middle-class men were voteless or registered under some other heading
	- Upwards of five million disenfranchised males existed before 1914, with at least 90% being working-class
- The complex property-based pre-1918 parliamentary franchise made it essential for political parties to have full-time agents, which the Labour Party lacked before the war, having only 17 agents in 1912

#### Labour Party Performance
- Labour's parliamentary performance was not indicative of its true level of pre-war appeal due to the franchise, financial restrictions, and the absence of full-time agents
- Party made significant headway in some constituencies, such as Holmfirth and Crewe, and its vote rose in areas such as Keighley.
- Local election successes rose from 82 in 1909 to 171 in 1913, and the party held around 500 of the 8k municipal seats on the eve of war

#### New Liberalism
- Led by figures like **T. Hobhouse**, **J. A. Hobson**, and **David Lloyd George**.
- Attempted to retain working-class support within the Liberal Party by offering social reforms and compromises
- The impact of it on the rise of Labour is debated, with some arguing it was responsible for Liberal revival pre-WW1, while others suggest Old Liberalism was more significant in certain regions, such as Wales and West Yorkshire
- New Liberalism had limited impact outside of Lancashire and Westminster, with many regional and local Liberal parties remaining committed to Old Liberal values
- Social reforms carried little weight among working-class voters - they instead prioritised job security, fair wages, and trade union rights.
- Its failure to create a base within the national Liberal Party or in regional areas contributed to its limited influence

#### Liberalism and Labour
- Liberals had a complex relationship with Labour and trade unions. Some historians argued New Liberalism failed to appeal to working-class voters and ultimately contributed to the decline of the Liberal Party
- Its inability to reconcile the demands of organised labour with those of capitalism, and its failure to halt the aspirations of politically active working-class members, led to a loss of trade union support.
- Taff Vale case and Osborne judgement further exacerbated the situation, ultimately strengthening the case for independent parliamentary representation for the working classes.
#### Regional Variations
- Surveys have demonstrated a variety of experiences
- Lancashire Liberalism revived on a diet of New Liberalism
- Welsh, north-east and Yorkshire Liberalism survived on Old Liberal values and support
- ILP and Labour made significant inroads into areas where Liberalism had once been dominant, such as Bradford, Halifax and Leicester
- Patchwork of experience is not open to easy interpretation, with most attention still focused on parliamentary politics

#### Labour Party growth
- Substantial local labour growth, with significant gains in areas such as West Yorkshire, despite the Liberal Party's strong presence
- Success in local contests cannot be dismissed as a natural result of increased intervention
- Municipal representation increased in towns like Bradford and Leeds, with the number of Lab councillors rising from 11 to 20 in Bradford between 1906 and 1913
- Liberals struggled to absorb the organised working class within its caucus system
- Liberals' problem were largely a product of their own inability to respond to the needs of working-class voters, driving them to look towards Labour for representation of their sectional interests
#### Historical debate about Labour's rise
- Some aargue Labour was rising rapidly before the war due to class conflict and the formation of the Labour Party
- Others argue the resilient pre-war Liberal Party was destroyed by the war
- A more balanced approach is necessary, accepting WW1 was responsible for significant for political and social change, but also acknowledging the Liberals were finding great difficulty in containing Lab's pre-war challenge

#### More on Labour formation and growth
- Independent Labour Party was officially formed in 1891 in Bradford, leading to the rapid formation of similar bodies, particularly in Lancashire and Yorkshire
- Were 305 ILP branches in 1895 across the country
- Its formation culminated in the creation of the Labour Representation Committee (LRC) in 19000, which became Labour in 1906
- It experienced significant growth, increasing its membership to over one million by 1909. Helped by support of Trade Unions
- Offered an alternative focus of activity and its alliance with TUs distance Liberals from unions
- Labour's growth was also evident in local elections and parliamentary by-elections between 1910 and 1914

#### Liberalism's decline
- Rise of Labour contributed to the decline, as Lib struggled to adapt to the changing political landscape
- Its neglect of labour interests and its failure to respond to the growth of class politics ultimately led to its decline
- Liberals' realisation of its mistakes came too late and it was unable to recapture the left, leading to its loss of political ascendancy
- Labour's emergence and growth had become a significant factor in the transformation of progressive politics in Britain, with the process of change well established before WW1.
