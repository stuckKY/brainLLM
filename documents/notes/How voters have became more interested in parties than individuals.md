#### Development of a party-orientated electorate
- A key aspect of modern British government, with voters tending to vote for parties rather than individual candidates
- Power of cabinet over legislation and strong party influence in Party have contributed to this - the Commons is essentials a veto over the proposals of the party's leader
- Voting for parties began to begin in 1857-68, with their being a significant reduction in split voting rates
- Decline in split voting preceded a decline in dissidence in the Commons, suggesting a reason for the development of party discipline
#### Electoral Influence and Party Discipline
- Some scholars suggest cross-pressuring electoral influences may have contributed to split voting, but there is no firm basis for considering this significant.
- Electoral influence believed to have declined in 1860s and 1870s, providing a possible explanation for the decline
- Party discipline increased markedly from 1850s to 1890s including local party oragnisations loyal to the party leadership
- Increase in discipline may be explained by the fact voters began to base their decisions more on party affiliation, reducing the incentives for MPs to dissent
- Development of a mid-Victorian party-oriented electorate was based on the erosion of the powers of individual MPs, voters used their votes to determine party control
