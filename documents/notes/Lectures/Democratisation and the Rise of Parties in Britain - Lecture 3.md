### Lecture Summary
- Debates about whether elites passed democratising reforms for partisan gain, or to stave of threats from more substantial reforms

- The implications of electoral corruption and violence for arguments about the rise of programatic politics
#### Rise of politics of opinion and changing culture of elections
- Rituals of elections in the early-19th century (O'Gorman 1992)
	- Election rituals: 'carnival', 'chairing the member', social inversion. These communicate meaning between politicians and the people (not just electors)
	- Centred on the candidates (as individuals, not parties)
- Order a key contribution of 1832 Reform Act
	- E.g. clearly defined electoral geography (Spychal 2024)
- During c19th electoral process comes to seem more orderly and disciplined
	- Separation of political elite from the people could be seen as less democratic (Vernon 1993)
- Transformation by mid-c20th:
	- Orderly elections based on competition between parties based on programmes

**Industrialisation and democracy**
- Industrial and social transformation
	- Population expansion and move towards urban area and North
	- Growth of manufacturing relative to agriculture: an industrial working class
	- Technological development (including rail) and productivity growth
	- Expansion of the newspaper press (national and provincial) especially after removal of 'taxes on knowledge' e.g. stamp tax 1855
- Growth of working-class political organisation including mass movements for franchise extension
	- Esp. Chartism (1838-1848): Women's suffrage movement
- Modernisation theory
	- Focuses on documenting the growing demand for the vote associated with 'modernisation': increasing wealth, urbanisation, education and industrialisation
	- Changing class structure creates new interests in society, which explains
		- The growth of democracy
		- Social cleavages explain the emergence of new politics of opinion (Lipset and Rokkan 1967)
**Why did British Elites accept democracy?**
- British Democratisation: Existing elites accept demands for franchise extension: leading to gradual and largely peaceful (not revolutionary) process of democratisation (Moore 1966; Dahl 1971)
- Why did existing elites assent to income-based franchise extension?
	- Position 1: Low States? Search for partisan advantage (Cowling 1967; Himmlefarb 1966): 'The Tories were democratic, one might say, because they assumed that the demos was Tory.' (Himmlefarb wrt 1867)
	- Position2: Intense threat? 'Threat of revolutionary unrest': (Acemoglu and Robinson 2000; Dasgupta and Ziblatt 2015; Aidt and Franck 2015

#### How Suffrage Extension Leads to National Partisan Politics of Opinion
- Suffrage extension implies (local) politics of opinion:
	- Increases costs of local influence (e.g. bribery, violence etc.)
	- To seek election for HoC, candidates must rely on politics of opinion.
- MPs in HoC increasingly seek to make policy contributions leading to increase in demand to speak and participate
- Pressure on time in HoC leads to cabinet becoming dominant in legislative initiative
- MPs become more party centred (policy positioning increasingly determined by Gov policy - little possibility of, or benefit to, independence)
- Voters become more party centred: as a result of choice of MPs being increasingly nationally partisan, plus newspaper partisanship
#### Parties as Organisation: Debating their Role
- Rise of parties as (national) organisations
	- National Union of Conservative and Constitutional Associations (1867); National Liberal Federation (1877)
	- Early, influential study blames 'The Caucus' for blind loyalty of MPs (Ostrogorski 1901)
	- Cox (1987) argues that party organisation comes too late and is of the wrong form to explain the rise of partisanship in either government or the electorate
- Does local party organisation explain partisanship?
	- Mechanism bridging parties and mass electorate (Rix 2016)
	- Local party organisation required much earlier than national party organisation (to reduce electoral costs) and associated with partisanship at temporally and within constituencies (Kam and Newson 2021)
#### Electoral Corruption
**Democratic importance of eliminating corrupt practices**
- Democracy requires more than just voting, it also requires the fair conduct in elections
- Corrupt practices were widespread in c19th elections including bribery and election violence
	- Vote-buying and treating continue to be important illicit tactics after 1832 (Gash, 1953; Kam and Newson 2021; Stokes et al 2013)
	- Legislative attempts to eliminate corrupt practices with mixed success (Gash 1953; Rix 2008)
- Corrupt practices often represent non-programatic links between candidates and voters

**Electoral Violence and Partisanship**
- Evidence that electoral violence was used strategically by candidates
	- Use of 'hired roughs,' 'bullies', 'bludgeon-men', 'toughs', 'thugs' to: coerce electors to vote for a candidate, to block turnout from opposition supporters (including kidnap), disrupt opposition campaign events and/or defend similar tactics from opposition
- Election violence and particularly election violence with hired roughs increases
	- Overtime with initial rise of partisanship
	- More violence where and when parties are stronger (both in the electorate and in terms of organsation (party slates))
- Questions the connection of party campaigning and the necessity of a 'politics of opinion'
