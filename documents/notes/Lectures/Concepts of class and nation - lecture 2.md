#### Measuring class
![[Pasted image 20251022154153.png]]
- Many different ways to try and create a categorisation of class
- The A,B etc scheme of classification is quite widely used
- It is very difficult to define *why* the categories are the categories. There are no theoretical underpinnings.
- Some schemes have an explicit theoretical basis, some don't.

**What might 'class' and 'nation' be?**
- Might be 'folk' categories - categories we all know but without a theoretical basis. They may be important but have less important than scientific.
- Scientific categories - These have some basis in theoretical investigation. By observing a person you can say what class they are in. May, for example, be based on what occupation someone has.
	- Subjective depends on people's internal mental states, whereas objective are ones you can observe. You can also look at the relationships between them - does the class people think they are in match what they might be placed in based on their occupation. 
	- Gellner: *'Nationalism is not the awakening of nations to self-consciousness; it invents nations where they do not exist.'*
- Not a category at all:
	- Thompson: *'I do not see class as a 'structure' nor even as a 'category' but as something which happens... in human relationships.'*

#### Nationalism: 
See: [[Nationalism's key characteristics]] [[What is Nationalism?]] [[How nationalism has changed over time]]
Empirical
- Central contrast between antiquity of claims of nationalists and the modernity of nationalism
- Anderson: Imagined Communities
	- Nation as an imagined political community: 'It is imagined because members of even the smallest nation will never know most of their fellow members, meet them, or even hear of them, yet in the mind of each lives the image of their communion' (p.6)
- Gellner: Nations and Nationalism
	- Central feature of nationalism - relationship between national culture and state. There may be a shared culture at a national level which has a relationship with a state function.
	- Nationalism functional for modern society (and enables preservation of plurality)
**Ideological**
There have been developments in the inclusivity of the nation and changes over time. 
- Expansionist nationalism, for example, is often associated with Fascism. But there were also new forms of nationalism in the mid-late c20th, with Anti/Postcolonial as a challenge to imperialism
- The theories of nationalism have been extremely politically salient
