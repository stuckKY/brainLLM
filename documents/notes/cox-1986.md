[[How voters have became more interested in parties than individuals ]]
#### Victorian Electoral behaviour
- Approached through two main strands of quant work: examining voting of individual electors and using documentary evidence from double-member districts.
- T.J. Nossiter and J.C. Mitchell used documentary evidence from double-member districts to calculate the rate at which Victorian voters split their votes between parties
- Nossiter found that in the 1860s there was no tendency for voters to split their votes less frequently.
- Mitchell found "a secular trend toward firmer party voting over time"
- The article uses two statistics: the split-voting rate and non-partisan plumping rate, to examine the degree to which voters base their decisions on attitudes towards parties
#### Non-partisan voting trends
- Split voting rates declined sharply from 19pc in 1857 to 5.5pc in 1868, and continued to decline there 58 to 86, fluctuating after but remaining below 1868 level
- Changes similar for all regions, with a decline to 1841, a reinstatement of high rates in 1857-57, a sharp decline from 1857-1868 and a further but small and gradual decline thereafter
- Non-partisan voting rate showed a similar pattern of change over time, with declines after each expansion of the electorate. 
	- Electoral strategies shifted from personal influence and patronage to mass appeals based on policy commitment
- Similar trends across counties, large and small boroughs and various regions of the country. Suggests they were national in scope
#### Party Affiliations and Voting Behaviour
- Urban constituencies that remained double-member after 1885 were particularly likely to have Labour or Lib/Lab
- Trend towards firmer party voting is reinforced when labour is a separate party, with less two-against-one and two-against two contests after 1900
- Mix of types of contest does not affect the basic patterns in the data, with trends in split voting (SV) and non-partisan plumping (NPP) preserved in all categories of contest
	- Decline in NPV from 1857-1865 may be attributed to voters being more party-orientated, possible due to the removal of Stamp Tax on newspapers in 1856 and the subsequent rise of a cheap, mass-circulation, partisan press.
	- Loss of policy initiative by backbench MPs may also have contributed to voters becoming more party orientated. MPs became more bound by party discipline and less able to pursue independent policies.
#### Decline of Backbench MPs' influence
- Significant in 19th century, Hoc changed from relatively small active membership to a more formalised system where the executive had greater control
- Backbenchers' rights to initiate and debate legislation, as well as to claim a hearing and raise debates, were eroded over time
- Also extended to distributive policy, with areas such as tariffs, civil service patronage, and local improvement bills being largely shut off from their influence
- Private bill committes were staffed with impartial members, with MPs being required to sign a declaration that they had no local or personal interest, limiting backbenchers' services on local interests
- By the mid-c19th, individual MPs became relatively unimportant. Elections focused on party affiliation and ability to deliver policy through party membership
